Overview

Senate Bill 315 creates the Artificial Intelligence Safety Measures Act. It establishes transparency, auditing, and incident reporting obligations for companies developing the most advanced AI models — often called "frontier models."

The law is widely considered one of the strongest state AI safety measures passed in the United States. It builds on similar 2025 legislation in California and New York but adds a first-in-the-nation requirement for annual independent third-party audits of AI safety practices.

Core Goal

Require large AI developers to be transparent about catastrophic risks and to have their safety claims independently verified.

Bipartisan Support

Passed the House unanimously (110-0) and Senate 52-5. Multiple Republican co-sponsors.

Who It Applies To

Covered Entities

Large Frontier Developers

Definition
  • Entities that train (or begin training) a "frontier model"
  • Frontier models = exceptionally large compute + high capability
  • Examples: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI, and similar
Key Thresholds
• High-compute training runs
• Models with potential for catastrophic risk
• "Large" developers (specific quantitative thresholds defined in the bill)

Key Requirements

Incident Reporting & Whistleblower Protections

Critical Safety Incident Reporting
  • Frontier developers must report critical safety incidents to the Illinois Emergency Management Agency & Office of Homeland Security.
  • 72-hour deadline after discovery (standard).
  • 24-hour deadline if the incident poses imminent risk of death or serious physical harm.
Whistleblower Protections
  • Strong protections for employees who report safety concerns in good faith.
  • Internal reporting processes required at covered companies.
  • Amends the Illinois Whistleblower Act to explicitly cover violations of this law.
  • Prohibits retaliation.

Key Details
  • • Administered by Illinois Emergency Management Agency (IEMA) and Office of Homeland Security in consultation with the Attorney General.
  • • No private right of action (individuals cannot sue directly under this law).
  • • Certain information is exempt from FOIA disclosure to protect sensitive safety data.
  • • The state may issue rules and guidance.
